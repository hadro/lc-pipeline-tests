 Money Market.
 TUESDAY, APRIL 29, —5 P. M.
 The stock market continued firm at quotations cur-
 rent yesterday. The sales were to a moderate extent
 only, and the tendency is prices evidently upward.
 Money is exceedingly easy, and will, without doubt, be
 easier still by the 1st of May, when the Banks are at li-
 berty again to expand.

 ½. Long Island Rail Road ¼. Ohio sixes ’56, Farmers’ Loan,

 The steamship Sarah Sands was telegraphed at the
 close of the afternoon, and her news came to hand just
 before dark, late as commercial accounts do not amount
 to much; they are, however, favorable. The Money,
 Corn and Cotton Markets were very firm, and all indica-
 tions of a further fall in the price of breadstuffs has
 disappeared. According to reports in the streets the

 The demand for foreign exchange has not been so
 active during the past two days as was anticipated.
 Some uneasiness still exists, and the probability is
 that a decline will be submitted to before the close of
 the market for the packet. There is a large supply of
 first class sterling bills in the market, but as there is no
 amount of remittances from importers and other opera-
 rators having partially abandoned that business for the
 present, we do not see what can sustain existing quota-
 tions.

 The Cambria brought back some bills protested for
 non-acceptance, drawn upon shipments of breadstuffs
 from this port. It appears that the bills were drawn
 upon a house in Hamburg, and sent out by the steamer
 from Boston. The Hamburg bank retains the bills or
 lading in time to protect the bills, but on account of some
 difficulty in the transportation of the mails between
 Hamburg and Boston, the bills remained protested,
 and consequently the bills which exchange were protested
 for non-acceptance, and returned by the Cambria.
 The only difficulty experienced is the delay, and the effect of
 the faux pas upon public confidence.
 Domestic Exchange continues very inactive, and our
 quoted rates cannot be considered other than nominal.
 A movement is on foot to induce the English creditors
 the North American Trust Company, to become
 purchasers of assets to an amount sufficient to induce
 them to discontinue farther litigations, the payments to
 hands of the receiver, now amounting to one and a
 half million of dollars, to be ratably divided. The
 property at present yields an income of about $80,000
 per annum—a proportion of income very superior, but
 in proper ownership, could be made to yield an annual
 interest of $100,000. Letters will be forwarded by the
 steamer of the 1st proximo, to London, inviting a con-
 versation with one of the non-resident creditors.
 No claim thus far, has been adjusted by Mr. David
 Levitt, that has not been fully proved to be legal one.
 The whole amount of claims, it is believed, that can be
 legally established, will not amount to over two hundred
 thousand dollars. If the creditors would harmonize
 with the stockholders, more than fifty cents of the re-
 spective interest could be divided. An amiable ar-
 rangement would, doubtlessly, be effected, but for the
 maneuvring of lawyers here, to keep alive hopes of
 results favorable to creditors, which they know can
 never be realised.
